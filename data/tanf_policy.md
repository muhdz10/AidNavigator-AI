# Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF)

## Program Overview

Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF) is a federally funded, state-administered program that provides temporary financial assistance and supportive services to low-income families with children. Created by the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996, TANF replaced the former Aid to Families with Dependent Children (AFDC) program. Each state operates its own TANF program under broad federal guidelines, which means eligibility rules, benefit amounts, and program names vary significantly by state.

## Common State Program Names

- California: CalWORKs
- New York: Family Assistance (FA)
- Texas: TANF Cash Assistance
- Florida: Temporary Cash Assistance (TCA)
- Illinois: TANF Cash
- Pennsylvania: TANF Cash Assistance
- Ohio: Ohio Works First (OWF)

## Federal Eligibility Requirements

### Basic Criteria

1. **Family composition**: Must include a minor child (under 18, or under 19 if a full-time student) OR a pregnant woman in her last trimester.
2. **Income**: Family income must fall below the state-set income threshold, which varies by state but is generally well below the Federal Poverty Level.
3. **Residency**: Must be a resident of the state where applying.
4. **Citizenship**: Must be a U.S. citizen, U.S. national, or qualified alien. Most qualified immigrants must wait 5 years after obtaining qualified status before receiving TANF.
5. **Resources/Assets**: Many states impose asset limits, typically $1,000–$3,000 in countable resources.

### Income Limits

Income thresholds vary dramatically by state. Examples of maximum monthly income for a family of 3:

| State | Approximate Monthly Income Limit | Monthly Benefit Amount |
|-------|--------------------------------|----------------------|
| California | $1,419 | $950 |
| New York | $1,062 | $789 |
| Texas | $401 | $308 |
| Mississippi | $680 | $260 |
| Massachusetts | $1,097 | $738 |
| Alaska | $1,640 | $1,145 |

*Note: These are approximate values and subject to annual adjustment. Most states set income limits significantly below the federal poverty level.*

## Work Requirements

Federal law requires states to ensure a minimum percentage of TANF families are engaged in "work activities":

### Hours Required

- **Single-parent families**: At least **30 hours per week** of work or work-related activities
- **Single parents with a child under age 6**: At least **20 hours per week**
- **Two-parent families**: At least **35 hours per week** (combined)

### Qualifying Work Activities

1. Unsubsidized or subsidized employment
2. On-the-job training
3. Community service programs
4. Job search and job readiness assistance (limited to 6–12 weeks)
5. Vocational educational training (limited to 12 months)
6. Providing child care for community service participants
7. Education directly related to employment (for those without a high school diploma)

### Exemptions from Work Requirements

States may exempt individuals from work requirements due to:
- Disability or incapacity
- Caring for a disabled family member
- Domestic violence (under the Family Violence Option)
- Pregnancy (particularly in the last trimester)
- Age (over 60 in some states)
- Caring for a very young child (age varies by state, typically under 1 year)

### Sanctions

Failure to comply with work requirements without good cause can result in:
- Reduction in benefits
- Removal of the noncompliant adult from the grant
- Termination of the entire family's benefits (in some states)

## Time Limits

- **Federal lifetime limit**: Families cannot receive federally funded TANF cash assistance for more than **60 cumulative months (5 years)** over an adult recipient's lifetime.
- **State limits**: Many states impose shorter time limits (e.g., 24, 36, or 48 months). Some states set a consecutive time limit (e.g., 24 months in a 60-month period).
- **Hardship extensions**: States may extend benefits beyond the 60-month limit for up to 20% of their caseload based on hardship.

## Supportive Services

Beyond cash assistance, TANF programs typically offer:
- Child care assistance
- Transportation assistance
- Job training and employment services
- Education support (GED, vocational programs)
- Substance abuse treatment
- Domestic violence services
- Emergency assistance

## Required Documentation

To apply for TANF, applicants typically need:
1. Proof of identity for all household members
2. Social Security numbers for all household members
3. Birth certificates for children in the household
4. Proof of income (pay stubs, benefit letters, self-employment records)
5. Proof of resources/assets (bank statements)
6. Proof of residency (lease, utility bill, mail)
7. Proof of citizenship or eligible immigration status
8. School enrollment verification for children over compulsory school age
9. Documentation of disability (if claiming exemption from work requirements)
10. Child care arrangements documentation

## How to Apply

- Contact your local Department of Social Services or Department of Human Services
- Apply online through your state's benefits portal
- Visit a local TANF office in person
- Call your state's benefits hotline

## Important Notes

- TANF is designed as temporary assistance — the goal is to help families achieve self-sufficiency through employment.
- States have broad flexibility in designing their TANF programs, so rules vary significantly.
- Receiving TANF may make family members eligible for other programs (categorical eligibility for SNAP, Medicaid, etc.).
- Two-parent families are eligible in all states, though some states have more restrictive rules for these families.
- Teen parents may be required to live with an adult and attend school as a condition of receiving benefits.
