# Medicaid — Health Coverage for Low-Income Individuals and Families

## Program Overview

Medicaid is a joint federal and state program that provides health coverage to millions of Americans, including eligible low-income adults, children, pregnant women, elderly adults, and people with disabilities. Medicaid is administered by states according to federal requirements. The program is the largest source of health coverage in the United States.

## Eligibility Categories

### Adults in Medicaid Expansion States

Under the Affordable Care Act (ACA), states have the option to expand Medicaid to cover nearly all adults under age 65 with household incomes up to **138% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL)**. As of 2025, 40 states plus Washington D.C. have adopted Medicaid expansion.

- **Income limit**: Up to 138% FPL (approximately $21,597/year for an individual; $44,367/year for a family of 4)
- **No asset test**: Expansion group eligibility is based on Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) only
- **No categorical requirement**: Adults ages 19–64 do not need to be parents, pregnant, or disabled to qualify

### Adults in Non-Expansion States

In the 10 states that have not expanded Medicaid, eligibility for adults is much more limited:
- Parents/caretaker relatives may qualify at very low income levels (varies by state, often below 50% FPL)
- Childless adults generally do not qualify regardless of income
- This creates a "coverage gap" where people earn too much for traditional Medicaid but too little for Marketplace subsidies

### Children (Medicaid and CHIP)

- Children in families with incomes up to at least **133% FPL** are covered by Medicaid in all states
- Many states cover children at higher income levels through the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP), often up to 200-300% FPL
- Infants are covered up to at least **185% FPL** in most states

### Pregnant Women

- Eligible at a minimum of **133% FPL** in all states
- Many states extend coverage to 185% FPL or higher
- Coverage includes prenatal care, labor and delivery, and postpartum care (up to 12 months postpartum in many states)

### Elderly and Disabled (SSI-Related)

- Individuals receiving Supplemental Security Income (SSI) automatically qualify for Medicaid in most states
- Additional pathways exist for people with specific disabilities
- Asset tests may apply: typically $2,000 for individuals, $3,000 for couples
- Medically Needy / Spend-Down programs in some states allow people with higher incomes to qualify after deducting medical expenses

## Covered Services

Medicaid covers a broad range of services including:
- Doctor visits and hospital stays
- Prescription drugs
- Laboratory and X-ray services
- Preventive care and screenings
- Mental health and substance use disorder services
- Home health services
- Nursing facility services (for those who qualify)
- Transportation to medical appointments

## Required Documentation

To apply for Medicaid, applicants typically need:
1. Proof of identity (birth certificate, passport, or state-issued ID)
2. Proof of U.S. citizenship or eligible immigration status
3. Social Security number
4. Proof of income (pay stubs, tax returns, W-2 forms)
5. Proof of residency in the state
6. Proof of household size / family composition
7. Proof of pregnancy (if applicable)
8. Documentation of disability (if applying through disability pathway)

## How to Apply

- Online through HealthCare.gov or your state's Medicaid website
- In person at your local Department of Social Services
- By phone through your state's Medicaid hotline
- By mail using your state's application form
- Through a certified application counselor or navigator

## Important Notes

- Medicaid eligibility is determined by your state of residence; rules vary significantly between states.
- There is no enrollment period for Medicaid — you can apply at any time of year.
- If denied, you have the right to appeal the decision.
- Medicaid coverage can be retroactive up to 3 months before the month of application if you would have been eligible during that time.
- Most Medicaid beneficiaries pay no premiums or copayments, though some states charge small amounts.
